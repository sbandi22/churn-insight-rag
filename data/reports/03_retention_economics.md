# Retention Economics and Win-Back Strategy (Synthetic Excerpt)

**Source:** Synthetic Industry Benchmark Report, "The Economics of Customer Retention" (2024)

## The Cost of Churn

Across subscription industries, acquiring a new customer costs roughly five to
seven times more than retaining an existing one. A widely cited rule of thumb is
that improving retention by 5 percentage points can increase profits by 25% to
95%, because retained customers buy more over time, cost less to serve, and refer
others. For this reason, even modest churn reductions usually deliver a higher
marginal return than equivalent spend on acquisition.

## Highest-Risk Segments

The highest-risk customer segments are remarkably consistent across SaaS and
telecom: new customers in their first 90 days (onboarding risk), customers on
month-to-month or no-commitment plans, customers who have logged a recent
unresolved support or billing complaint, customers whose usage or engagement has
declined month-over-month, and customers reaching the end of a promotional or
introductory pricing period. Enterprise accounts that lose their internal
champion are a distinct high-severity segment: low in frequency but very high in
revenue impact.

## Win-Back and Prevention

Prevention outperforms win-back: re-acquiring a churned customer typically
succeeds only 15-30% of the time and often at a discount that erodes margin.
The most cost-effective programs combine predictive churn scoring with tiered
interventions: automated nudges for low-value at-risk customers, human
customer-success outreach for mid-value accounts, and executive engagement plus
tailored commercial offers for high-value enterprise accounts. Measuring
incremental impact with holdout control groups is essential, since many "saved"
customers would have stayed regardless of the intervention.
